I've been thinking about this story of the girl who returned from England to
colonial North Carolina interred in a barrel of rum. It goes that the girl had
developed a fascination with the homeland of her parents, where she was born,
before her memory. She convinced them to bring her there to see it. Only the
father brought her.  He promised to the mother that they would return together.

When she died on the return voyage, rather than bury her at sea, her father
convinced the ship captain to allow him to preserve her in a barrel of rum.
Thus, he fulfilled his promise to his wife. It is not likely that she found
this satisfactory.

I've been thinking about this grave, how it is stacked with dolls, plush
animals, toy cars and trucks, games, costume jewelry, all of it piled and
scattered over the tomb. What is that about?

Do children leave this stuff as a kind of empathic gesture? Do parents who
lose children leave something here that the lost child had liked?

There is probably an answer. What I think? Children who die occupy a special
part of heaven. I'm fortunate not to know this personally. I think there is
certainly something different about losing a child than about losing a friend,
parent, relation or a lover. There is a sense of incompleteness, of unrealized
hopes.  It isn't a mistake to say that part of what we feel for children is the
hope and possibility that they represent as a person in the future, as an
adult.

Usually, as adolescents, we already disappoint; however, dying as children--
this leaves a bright future and an innocent present cut off, cut short,
unrealized and unfulfilled.

This strange display of the things that our culture manufactures to entertain
children, what does it express in this context? If a child leaves something
they imagine that this child still lives somewhere in the afterlife to
appreciate it and be entertained by it. If a parent who loses a child leaves
something, it is an expression of the heaven shared by children who die.
In the hearts of the adults who remember them, they are forever someone
adorable and adored in their innocence and future promise.
